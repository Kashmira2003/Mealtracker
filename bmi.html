What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical measure that relates an individual's weight to their height, providing a simple and quick screening tool to assess whether a person has a healthy body weight. BMI is widely used in both clinical and public health settings to categorize individuals into different weight status categories, helping to identify potential health risks associated with being underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese.
The formula for calculating BMI is straightforward and involves dividing a person's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters. For those using pounds and inches, a slightly modified formula is employed, multiplying the weight in pounds by 703 before dividing by the square of the height in inches.
The World Health Organization (WHO) and many health agencies worldwide have established standard BMI categories based on extensive population studies. These categories include:
1.Underweight (BMI less than 18.5): Individuals falling into this category may face health risks associated with insufficient body weight. Underweight can be indicative of malnutrition, eating disorders, or other underlying health issues.
2.Normal weight (BMI 18.5 to 24.9):This range is considered healthy for most adults. People within this category generally have a lower risk of weight-related health problems.
3.Overweight (BMI 25 to 29.9): Individuals in this category have a higher amount of body fat than is considered healthy, which may increase the risk of various health conditions, including cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes.
